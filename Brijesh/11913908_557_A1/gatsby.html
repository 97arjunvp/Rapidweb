The Great Gatsby

by F. Scott Fitzgerald

The Great Gatsby, written by F. Scott Fitzgerald, is a classic American novel set in the Roaring Twenties. It tells the story of Jay Gatsby, a mysterious millionaire known for his extravagant parties and unrequited love for Daisy Buchanan. Narrated by Nick Carraway, a Yale graduate and Gatsby's neighbor, the novel explores themes of decadence, idealism, and social upheaval. Gatsby's obsession with recreating the past and his pursuit of the American Dream ultimately lead to his tragic downfall. The novel vividly captures the spirit of the Jazz Age, highlighting the moral decay beneath the glittering surface of wealth and privilege. Characters such as Tom Buchanan, Daisy's brutish husband, and Jordan Baker, a cynical golfer, add depth to this exploration of love, ambition, and betrayal. Fitzgerald's lyrical prose and keen social commentary make The Great Gatsby a timeless reflection on the American experience, illustrating the illusory nature of dreams and the destructive power of obsession. The novel remains a poignant critique of the American Dream, revealing the hollow pursuit of wealth and status in a rapidly changing society.
